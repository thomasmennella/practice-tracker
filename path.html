How to Use This Map
▾

This page tracks two complementary frameworks for orienting in practice. They are not two separate tracks — they are two ways of mapping the same territory.

The Seven Purifications — what you cultivate

The purifications are the conditions you deliberately establish. You train sīla, you develop samādhi, you take up vipassanā practice systematically. Agency lives here. The first two purifications — sīla-visuddhi and citta-visuddhi — are entirely within your hands: conduct and concentration. The later purifications follow from these conditions when they are sufficiently established.

The Sixteen Insight Knowledges — what the mind sees

The insight knowledges are not techniques you do — they are what happens when the conditions are sufficiently in place. You do not practice toward udayabbaya-ñāṇa. You practice ānāpānasati, and if the conditions are right, udayabbaya-ñāṇa arises. Trying to deliberately produce the insight knowledges is one of the more reliable ways to obstruct them.

How they relate

Purification 5 (paṭipadā-ñāṇadassana-visuddhi) is the insight knowledges — ñāṇas 1 through 13 are what that purification consists of. The later purifications are defined by the arising of the insight knowledges. The two maps are not parallel lists; they overlap and fold into each other.

The practical upshot: Your work is P1 and P2 — conduct and samādhi. If samādhi deepens sufficiently, the insight knowledges will arise on their own schedule. Mark what you have genuinely experienced with some consistency. The map is not the territory.

Mark stages that genuinely characterize your practice. These are conditions you establish — P1 (sīla) and P2 (samādhi) are your primary work. P3 onward arise when the earlier foundations are stable. Not linear checkboxes.

Sixteen Insight Knowledges (Vipassanā-ñāṇa)

These are not things you practice toward — they are what the mind sees when conditions are right. Mark only what has arisen with clarity and some stability, not what arose once or what you hope is present. Aspiration and recognition are different things.

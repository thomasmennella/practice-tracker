Recognizing Concentration Depth — Cross-Reference Guide ▾

Use this after a sit to cross-reference what you experienced. Not a checklist to consult during practice.

Momentary concentration

Attention lands and wanders repeatedly. You keep returning. No sustained hold. This is the ordinary starting point — nothing is wrong.

Access concentration (upacāra-samādhi)

Hindrances have quieted but not vanished. Mind settles but remains aware of sounds, body, surroundings. Attention stays on the breath for extended stretches without effort. A sign or light impression of the breath may appear. You could be interrupted and return. The mind is collected but not absorbed.

First absorption (first jhāna) — 4 factors present together

  • Applied attention (vitakka) — You actively place attention on the breath. Like pressing a piano key. You notice yourself choosing to return.
  • Sustained attention (vicāra) — Attention stays without constant re-application. Like holding the key down. Breath holds you for multiple cycles without effort.
  • Rapture (pīti) — Physical pleasurable energy: tingles, goosebumps, waves up the spine, involuntary smiling, tears of joy, body feeling electrically alive. Unmistakable when strong. You did nothing to create it.
  • Pleasure (sukha) — Calm, sweet, settled happiness. Softer than rapture. Like sinking into a warm bath. Zero desire to move or stop. Sitting feels profoundly pleasant.

All four present together, sustained 10+ minutes. Breath becomes subtle, may nearly disappear. Time distortion common. Hindrances completely suppressed — not reduced, gone.

Second absorption — 3 factors

Applied and sustained attention drop away — you are no longer "doing" anything. Rapture and pleasure merge into stable, unified joy. Less fizzy, more like rapture dissolved into thick honey. One-pointedness (ekaggatā): attention locked — not watching the breath, you are the breath experience. No meditator watching. Effort has completely ceased.

Third absorption — 2 factors

Rapture is gone. What remains: exquisite ease without excitement — like floating in space. Equanimity (upekkhā) and one-pointedness only. Perfect comfort. You could stay indefinitely. Breathing may become imperceptible.

Fourth absorption — 2 factors

Even pleasure is gone. Pure, neutral balance. Clear, luminous awareness. Nothing pleasant, nothing unpleasant — pure equanimity and one-pointedness. Breathing typically stops or becomes undetectable. Profound stillness and purity.

Honest cross-reference: Brief rapture or pleasant energy during a sit is not first absorption. Threshold means all four factors are locked together in stable unity for 10+ minutes, with hindrances fully absent. When in doubt, you have not crossed the threshold — and that is fine.

The Three Signs (Nimitta) — A Guide ▾

As concentration deepens in breath meditation, a mental image of the breath tends to arise on its own. It passes through three stages. Recognition is for after the sit.

Preparatory sign (parikamma nimitta)

A faint, unstable image or impression — fuzzy, dim, flickering. No fixed shape. Depends entirely on the physical breath; dissolves when attention wavers. You are still holding it together. This is the ordinary, correct starting point.

Acquired sign (uggaha nimitta)

The same image, now steadied. It holds its shape clearly and no longer requires the physical breath to remain — the breath can recede while the sign persists. You are no longer feeding it effortfully. The steadiness is usually unmistakable; the absence of doubt is itself part of the marker.

Counterpart sign (paṭibhāga nimitta)

The image becomes luminous and fully stable. The mind sinks into it and rests there without strain or maintenance. This is the doorway to access concentration and, beyond it, absorption.

Five Hindrances
Five Hindrances (Pañca Nīvaraṇā) — A Guide ▾

The five hindrances are the primary obstacles to samādhi. They are not failures — they are the texture of pre-jhāna practice. Identifying them clearly is itself part of the work.

1. Sensual desire (kāmacchanda)

Craving for pleasant experience — a sound, a fantasy, a plan. Recognizable as restless reaching or distraction toward what seems preferable to what is here. Antidote: returning attention firmly to the breath.

2. Ill-will (byāpāda)

Aversion, irritation, resistance — the push-away to desire's pull. Can appear as frustration with the sit, annoyance at sounds, or a low-grade contracted quality. Antidote: mettā toward the source of resistance, or noticing the aversion without feeding it.

3. Sloth and torpor (thīna-middha)

Thīna is sluggishness of mind; middha is heaviness of body. Together: dullness, loss of clarity, half-sleep. Distinct from the stillness of deep samādhi — torpor has no clarity or alertness. Antidote: brightening attention, sitting upright, open-eyed practice.

4. Restlessness and worry (uddhacca-kukkucca)

Uddhacca is agitation — the mind jumping, evaluating, comparing. Kukkucca is remorse or worry. Together they prevent settledness. Restlessness often masquerades as diligence — checking whether the sit is going well is itself uddhacca.

5. Doubt (vicikicchā)

Skeptical indecision about the path, the method, or one's own capacity. Distinct from genuine inquiry — it paralyzes rather than investigates. Antidote: recollecting the Triple Gem and the accounts of those who have walked this path.

The hindrances are suppressed — not eradicated — by concentration. Their permanent abandonment comes through insight practice at the appropriate stages of the path.
Insight & Phenomena
Insight Knowledges & Phenomena — A Guide ▾

These are not stages to pursue — they are what the mind sees when conditions are sufficiently established. Log what was genuinely present, not what you hope was present.

Three characteristics

Anicca — Directly perceiving the arising and passing of phenomena moment to moment. Not the concept of impermanence but seeing it happen.
Dukkha — The pressured, unsatisfactory quality of conditioned experience — the subtle stress underlying even pleasant states.
Anattā — The absence of a controller behind what is happening. Attention moves without a "mover." Particularly striking when noticed without seeking it.

Insight knowledges (vipassanā-ñāṇa)

Arising & passing (udayabbaya) — Rapid, clear perception of phenomena arising and ceasing. May arrive with unusual clarity or energy. A significant marker.
Dissolution (bhaṅga) — Only the passing side is prominent; phenomena vanish as soon as they appear.
Fearfulness / danger / disenchantment — The natural response to clearly seeing impermanence. Not depression — a lucid seeing of the instability of formations.
Equanimity toward formations — Deep, balanced stillness. Neither grasping nor pushing away. The most mature pre-path insight knowledge.

Unusual phenomena

Luminosity, body movements, surges of rapture (pīti), spatial distortion, cessation of breath, or profound stillness may arise. These are byproducts of deepening practice — not goals, not problems. Note them without amplifying them.

The ten corruptions of insight (vipassanūpakkilesa) — illumination, rapture, bliss, and others — can be mistaken for attainment. Their arising is not the problem; attachment to them is. A teacher's guidance is most valuable precisely here.
